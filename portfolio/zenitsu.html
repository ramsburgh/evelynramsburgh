Zenitsu — Usability Testing for an Internal Developer Platform

Zenitsu streamlined how teams generated, configured, and deployed modular web apps at Sales Systems. This case study focuses on late-stage usability testing and how the findings shaped the next iteration.

Category Enterprise · Internal Platform
Focus Usability Testing · UX/UI
Tools Carbon v10 · Sketch · Prototyping

Overview

Zenitsu was built to reduce the overhead of creating and deploying scalable internal applications. The platform combined four components: a core generator, shared libraries, a user interface for configuration, and a DevOps process that automated setup and deployment. By standardizing the workflow, Zenitsu aimed to decrease avoidable errors, shorten time-to-ship, and make module ownership easier to manage across teams.

Users

Zenitsu served a diverse internal audience: developers who created and maintained modules, product owners who shaped requirements, administrators who managed environments and access, and analysts/support roles who relied on predictable structures to troubleshoot. To keep the platform grounded in real workflows, we ran recurring feedback sessions and synthesized learnings into personas and journey artifacts.

What I Led

I led design efforts on the user interface and interaction patterns, aligning the experience with Carbon Design System v10. While Zenitsu covered multiple technical layers, my responsibility was to ensure the UI made complex configuration feel clear, predictable, and safe—especially in steps where users defined schemas, roles, and deployment targets.

Why Testing

For this case study, I’m intentionally not deep-diving into early discovery (interviews, ideation, or wireframing). Instead, I’m focusing on late-stage validation: how we tested a high-fidelity prototype, what we learned, and which changes were prioritized as a result.

Goals

  • Validate user performance on core tasks (create, configure, deploy).
  • Identify friction points and design risks before implementation.
  • Measure perceived efficiency and satisfaction using a lightweight questionnaire.

Method

We ran moderated user sessions with a think-aloud protocol. Participants completed key flows across the prototype while describing their expectations, interpretations of terminology, and decision points. After tasks, we collected structured feedback and administered UMUX (a short usability metric designed as a quicker alternative to SUS for iterative evaluation).

Prototype Coverage

The test prototype represented a realistic late-stage experience. Because the UI leveraged Carbon and established internal patterns, we were able to prototype at high fidelity quickly and focus sessions on comprehension, task flow, and clarity—rather than UI novelty.

Key Screens Tested

We focused on the screens that carried the most decision-making weight:

  • Module list: discover and manage owned modules.
  • Create new module: guided flow to define endpoints, schema, UI choices, and deployment.
  • Status & deployment: confirm environment and understand outcomes.

Create Module — Flow Breakdown

The creation flow was intentionally structured as a sequence, not a single dense form, to reduce errors and help users confirm intent at each step.

  • Step 1 — Definition: module name, endpoint, and initial metadata.
  • Step 2 — Backend: schema definition using visual and schema views.
  • Step 3 — Frontend: UI configuration using Carbon-based components.
  • Step 4 — Deployment: environment selection and deploy confirmation.

Results

UMUX score landed at 64, which we treated as “acceptable with clear opportunities to improve.” More importantly, sessions revealed repeatable breakdowns: places where users paused, re-read, or needed confirmation before proceeding.

Key Findings

  • Reduce cognitive load on the homepage by removing low-value components.
  • Use tooltips intentionally for terminology and constraints, not as a substitute for clarity.
  • Support progressive disclosure: tutorials for new users, less guidance for experienced users.
  • Clarify backend roles and permissions to avoid misconfiguration.
  • Define Schema Editor capabilities explicitly (what it can/can’t do).
  • Use icons consistently in the Frontend step to reinforce meaning.
  • Make environment options in Deployment explicit and comparable.
  • Remove or defer non-actionable data in Status to reduce noise.

What Changed After Testing

Findings were translated into a prioritized improvement list for the next iteration. The emphasis was on reducing ambiguity in high-impact steps (schema, roles, deployment), simplifying surfaces that users used as “home base,” and introducing guidance that adapts to proficiency—so the product remains approachable without becoming noisy.

Conclusion

Zenitsu’s value came from making complex, multi-step application setup feel routine and reliable. By validating the late-stage prototype with real internal users, we surfaced the specific points where confidence dropped— and we had a clear roadmap to improve comprehension, reduce errors, and strengthen end-to-end flow before implementation.
