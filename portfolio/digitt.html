Digitt — Digital Signature Process & Core Debt-Reduction Flows

Digitt is a web platform that helps users reduce credit card debt by refinancing to a lower interest rate. I supported the early product (starting in 2018) by designing key user flows and screens—including the digital signature experience—focused on clarity, trust, and smooth completion of high-stakes steps.

Category Fintech · Debt Reduction
Role UX/UI Designer (Flows)
Focus IA · Forms · Digital Signature

About

Debt refinancing is a sensitive, high-friction domain: users need to understand what they are agreeing to, feel confident in the process, and complete steps without errors. In 2018, Digitt was scaling its early MVP into more robust digital processes. My work focused on the product’s most critical moments—especially multi-step forms and agreement flows—where small UX issues can create large drop-offs.

Over time, Digitt has grown and evolved. This case study documents my contributions to the earlier product stage and the UX decisions that supported user comprehension and completion.

Objective

Create a user-friendly web experience that simplifies the debt-reduction journey—from initial information capture to document review and digital signature—so users can confidently progress through a complex financial process with minimal friction.

Goals

  • Reduce cognitive load across multi-step forms and verification steps.
  • Clarify expectations: what is required, why it matters, and what happens next.
  • Design a signature flow that feels trustworthy, explicit, and easy to complete.
  • Provide implementation-ready UX specs to support fast iteration with engineering.

Process

The work was structured around a pragmatic, product-first approach: understand where users get stuck, simplify the sequence, make decisions visible, and validate with stakeholders. Because fintech experiences depend on trust, we prioritized transparency and clear microcopy at each step.

1) Research & User Understanding

  • Reviewed market patterns for debt consolidation and refinancing experiences.
  • Audited MVP data and stakeholder feedback to identify drop-off risks and unclear steps.
  • Aligned on the “trust checklist”: clarity, explicit consent, and error prevention.

2) User Flows & Information Architecture

I mapped end-to-end flows from first visit through application completion and signature—sequencing tasks to minimize back-and-forth and ensuring each step answered three questions: What is this? Why do you need it? What happens next?

  • Defined the minimal steps needed to gather financial information without overwhelming users.
  • Structured review moments (summaries) to prevent mistakes before submission.
  • Designed clear success and failure paths to keep users oriented.

3) Wireframes & Prototyping

I translated the flow into wireframes and interactive prototypes to validate layout, field grouping, and error prevention patterns. Prototypes allowed stakeholders to review the experience as a real journey—making gaps and ambiguities visible early.

4) Digital Signature Experience

The digital signature step is both a legal checkpoint and a trust moment. I designed the signature flow to make the user’s action explicit, reduce uncertainty, and ensure the interface supported careful review—not accidental consent.

  • Clear document review states (what you’re signing, what to confirm).
  • Progress indicators and “safe” navigation to avoid losing context.
  • Confirmation patterns that reinforce completion and next steps.

5) Iteration & Implementation Support

I worked closely with engineering to ensure the UX intent translated into the built experience, clarifying interactions, states, and edge cases. This included tightening error handling and ensuring users had clear recovery paths if something went wrong.

Results

The outcome was a streamlined set of flows that supported Digitt’s early growth: clearer form progression, more confident handoffs between steps, and a signature experience designed to reduce uncertainty in a high-stakes moment. The work helped establish a foundation that the team could iterate on as the product matured.

Scope & Evolution

Note: My contribution was focused on product UX—specifically user flows, information architecture, and key screens for form completion and signature. I did not own Digitt’s brand, design system, or long-term visual identity.

As Digitt evolved, parts of the visual design changed—including branding and component styling. At the time, some button color choices had limited contrast and were difficult to perceive. Improving contrast and accessibility was a consistent recommendation I raised with product. These elements were later updated, resulting in better accessibility for users.

Final Remarks

Designing fintech flows requires balancing speed with certainty. The experience must keep users moving while also helping them understand what they are providing and agreeing to. This work reinforced the value of clear sequencing, progressive disclosure, and accessibility advocacy—especially in moments where trust determines whether users complete the journey.
