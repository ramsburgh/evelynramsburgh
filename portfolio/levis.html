Levi’s — Mobile Workflow for Promoters (5-Day Design Sprint)

I designed the UI for a mobile application to support Levi’s in-store promoters during a five-day Design Sprint. The sprint compressed discovery, ideation, prototyping, and validation into one week—helping the team align quickly on a practical, brand-consistent workflow that could be implemented with confidence.

Category Retail · Mobile UX
Role UI Designer
Method 5-Day Design Sprint

About

Levi’s promoters operate in a fast-paced retail environment: they guide customers, answer fit and product questions, and often need to switch context quickly. This project explored how a lightweight mobile tool could reduce friction in day-to-day promoter work—without distracting from the in-person customer experience.

I partnered with Smarttie (a development agency I collaborate with as a freelance designer) as the UI Designer in the sprint team. My focus was translating the sprint’s decisions into a clear, implementable interface: screens, components, states, and handoff assets.

Objective

Design a promoter-first mobile experience that improves speed and confidence in-store—while staying aligned with Levi’s visual identity and supporting a smooth development handoff.

Goals

  • Create an intuitive workflow that supports promoters under time pressure.
  • Make key information scannable and actionable during customer interactions.
  • Reflect Levi’s brand through typography, tone, and visual consistency.
  • Deliver UI that is implementation-ready, including states and interaction guidance.

Design Sprint, in practice: a five-day framework to align on a problem, prototype a solution, and validate it with users—reducing risk before committing to build.

Process

The sprint structure gave the team a shared pace and a concrete set of decision points. My work followed the sprint rhythm: translating outputs into flows, tightening UI clarity, and preparing assets for testing and handoff.

1) Understand & Define

  • Mapped promoter activities, common questions, and operational constraints in-store.
  • Identified friction points that slow promoters down or add cognitive load mid-conversation.
  • Defined the minimum set of features needed to support a complete workflow.

2) Ideate

We generated and compared multiple interaction approaches—prioritizing speed of access, minimal taps, and a clear information hierarchy. Concepts were evaluated against two constraints: promoters need to stay present with customers, and the UI must remain legible at a glance.

3) Prototype

We moved quickly into a low-fidelity prototype to validate structure and navigation before committing to visual detail. To accelerate build feasibility and reduce ambiguity in components and patterns, we aligned on Material Design as the underlying UI framework.

4) Test

We conducted usability sessions with Levi’s promoters and asked participants to think aloud as they navigated the prototype. Feedback focused on terminology, task completion confidence, and whether each screen made the next step obvious. Findings were translated into immediate iteration opportunities—especially around navigation clarity and content hierarchy.

5) Design & Implementation

After validation, I produced high-fidelity UI that balanced Levi’s brand expression with the structure of Material components. I also collaborated closely with Smarttie’s developers to support a smooth handoff—providing assets, specs, and interaction notes to reduce rework and interpretation gaps.

Results

The sprint produced a validated mobile workflow that helped promoters complete key tasks faster and with less friction. The final UI prioritized legibility, predictable navigation, and brand consistency—resulting in a cohesive experience that supports both promoter efficiency and customer-facing confidence.

Key Learnings

  • Time-boxed decision points prevent over-design and keep teams aligned on outcomes.
  • Testing early—even with a simple prototype—quickly reveals terminology and hierarchy issues.
  • Aligning on a component system (Material Design) accelerates feasibility and clarifies implementation.
  • Close designer–developer collaboration reduces ambiguity and increases build quality under sprint timelines.
