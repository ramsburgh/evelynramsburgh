Case study #1

IBM Seller Incentives

A multi-year modernization of IBM Seller Incentives (formerly the Field Management System, FMS): migrating legacy UX from Northstar to Carbon, prioritizing UX debt, and improving navigation and satisfaction signals across a global sales organization (2020–2023).

Context Enterprise · Internal Platform
Role Senior Product Designer (UX/UI)
Team Multi-disciplinary UX + Product + Eng

Overview

IBM Seller Incentives is a core component of the IBM Global Sales Incentives (GSI) ecosystem. The platform helps sellers and sales managers understand incentive structures, track progress, and make decisions aligned with sales goals—at enterprise scale.

When I joined, the experience carried meaningful UX debt from years of iterative additions on a legacy architecture. At the same time, IBM was moving product surfaces toward Carbon, raising the bar for consistency, accessibility, and modern interaction patterns.

My Role

I worked as part of a multi-disciplinary UX team supporting product owners and engineering. My focus was pragmatic: identify the highest impact UX debt, translate improvements into Carbon-aligned patterns, and partner with developers to ship changes that were feasible within a constrained legacy codebase.

  • Led UX/UI improvements during the transition from Northstar to Carbon.
  • Partnered with Product Owners to clarify UX principles, scope, and decision criteria.
  • Worked closely with engineering to align on constraints and ship incremental wins.

The Challenge

The primary challenge was improving usability for sellers and sales managers while modernizing the UI. The platform’s legacy architecture made “perfect” redesigns unrealistic; the work demanded thoughtful prioritization, strong collaboration, and consistent delivery of smaller improvements that compounded over time.

Legacy Context

The platform was historically known as FMS (Field Management System) and accumulated navigation and information architecture issues. Some patterns were inconsistent, terminology drifted across areas, and key tasks required more effort than necessary—especially for infrequent users.

Signal: NPS Feedback

A recurring theme from users was that navigation felt “painful.” I used this feedback as a starting point to focus our evaluation on discoverability, wayfinding, and cognitive load rather than purely visual refresh.

Method: Heuristic Evaluation

To quickly identify navigation issues across the product, I ran a heuristic evaluation—reviewing key flows against established usability principles. The output was not a “report for the shelf”; it became a shared backlog of actionable UX debt items we could prioritize and ship.

Prioritization: Identifying UX Debt

The backlog was extensive, so the work started with triage. I grouped findings into a small set of categories that were easy for product and engineering to reason about, and used them to drive prioritization conversations during planning.

  • User interface inconsistencies and legacy components
  • Content, terminology, and messaging clarity
  • Information architecture and navigation structure
  • Accessibility gaps and contrast/legibility issues
  • Customer journey consistency across sections
  • Omnichannel continuity where applicable

Strategy: Ship the Highest-Impact Improvements

Given the legacy architecture, the strategy was incremental: prioritize changes that reduced friction for the most common tasks, improved wayfinding, and aligned UI patterns to Carbon—without destabilizing core workflows.

  • Modernize navigation patterns and page structure while preserving user recognition.
  • Reduce cognitive load by removing redundant content and clarifying labels.
  • Align components to Carbon to improve consistency and accessibility.
  • Partner with engineering early to confirm feasibility before investing in high-fidelity UI.

Impact

Over time, the work contributed to measurable sentiment improvement. While enterprise platforms rarely transform overnight, consistent UX debt reduction can meaningfully change how users perceive the product—especially around navigation and speed.

  • Achieved steady month-over-month improvement in Net Promoter Score (NPS), increasing by approximately 5–7 points per month during key periods.
  • Helped shift perception from one of the lowest-ranked experiences (initially around -10 to -15 NPS) toward a more positively received product (reaching ~10–20 NPS over time).
  • Enabled a smoother UX transition as the platform moved toward modern cloud-aligned patterns.
  • Improved application speed perceptions and addressed recurring navigation issues through iterative releases.

What I’d Highlight to a Hiring Team

This project is less about a single “hero redesign” and more about operating effectively inside real constraints: legacy systems, enterprise governance, and long-lived backlogs. The value was in consistently making the product easier to use—using defensible methods, clear prioritization, and strong collaboration.

  • Translating noisy feedback into actionable, prioritized UX work.
  • Driving alignment between design intent and engineering reality.
  • Improving consistency and accessibility through design system adoption.
  • Delivering incremental wins that compound into measurable impact.
